About

I am an hugely influential 18th-century Scottish political economist, philosopher and author, often honored as the "Father of Modern Economics" because of my ideas surrounding capitalism, free markets and the relationship between supply and demand. I am most notable for my work in my 1776 book, "The Wealth of Nations", the first major work surrounding the political economy. In this book, I argued against regulation of commerce and trade, and wrote that if people were set free to better themselves, it would produce economic prosperity for all, forming basic ideas surrounding capitalism.

Works in the Field of Economics


The Wealth of Nations, 1776


The core of Smith's thesis was that humans' natural tendency for self-interest would result in prosperity. Smith argued that by free trade and opening the markets up to domestic and foreign competition, people's natural self-interest would promote greater prosperity than could stringent government regulations.


This free-market force which Adam Smith called the invisible hand needed support to bring about its magic. In particular, the market that would emerge from increased division of labor would create mutual interdependencies which could promote social welfare through individual profit motives.
